## Safety and security

### **Landmines**

Croatia was declared ‘mine free’ in early 2026, which means that all known minefields have been cleared. Populated areas, major routes and tourist sites are clear of mines and are safe to visit. However, isolated areas in the mountains and countryside may not all have been cleared. In isolated and remote areas be careful not to stray from roads, paved areas or marked paths without an experienced guide and never enter areas that are taped off.

### **Crime**

Croatia is a relatively safe country. However, you should take sensible precautions when carrying passports and money in busy tourist areas.

* Don’t carry your credit card, travel tickets and money together - leave spare cash and valuables in a safe place.
* Don’t carry your passport unless absolutely necessary and leave a copy of your passport (and travel and insurance documents) with family or friends at home and email it to yourself to have available if needed.
* Take special care of your valuables if you’re travelling by train, especially at night.

### **Reporting Crime**

If you’re a victim of a crime while in Croatia, report it to the local police immediately. In particular if theft is involved you may need a police report for any insurance claim. You can contact us at the Irish Embassy in Zagreb if you need help.

### **Driving**

If you’re planning to drive in Croatia, you should exercise caution and please note that vehicles are driven on the right hand side of the road. If you want to drive, you should visit the [**Croatian Automobile Club’s website**](http://www.hak.hr/en), which is also useful for information on traffic flow and any disruptions, and follow these guidelines:

* Bring your full Irish driver’s licence and international driving permit and make sure you have adequate and appropriate insurance.
* Special seats are required for infants and children under twelve may not sit in the front seat of a car.
* For passenger cars, headlights are mandatory year-round during the night or when visibility is poor. Dipped headlights must be on at all times from 1 November to 31 March.
* Motorcycles and mopeds must have their lights on at all times throughout the year, regardless of weather conditions or time of day.
* Between 15 November and 15 April, you must have winter tyres on the driving axle and carry snow chains in your vehicle, and also during wintry conditions outside those times.
* You must also have a fluorescent vest in the car (not the boot) while driving and to wear it if you have to leave your vehicle at any stage (e.g. a breakdown).
* Under Croatian law, it is illegal for professional drivers (e.g. hauliers, taxi drivers, etc.) and young people (16-24 years) to drive with any alcohol in their system (there is also a zero tolerance policy for those in charge of yachts and boats). For other drivers, the blood/alcohol limit is 0.05.
* Police routinely spot check motorists for drinking and driving and will administer breath-analyser tests at the site of even the most minor accident. Drivers who refuse to submit to a breath analyser test are presumed to have been driving while intoxicated. Croatian police are obliged to take blood samples to test blood alcohol levels in the event of an accident resulting in death or serious injuries.

### **Hiring a Vehicle**

If you’re hiring a vehicle, we advise you not to hand over your passport as a form of security. If you’re allowing your passport to be photocopied, keep it in your sight at all times.

Check that you have adequate insurance and read the small print of the vehicle hire contract (particularly any waiver that will come into effect if the vehicle is damaged).

### **City of Dubrovnik: Restricted Traffic Zone**

The area around Dubrovnik’s Old Town is a restricted traffic zone from March 1 to November 30, as well as during special events or periods of increased traffic.

Only approved vehicles can enter, and **you must ensure approval before arrival**. You can submit vehicle details through a free process on the [**City of Dubrovnik Official Website**](https://www.dubrovnik.hr/zona-posebnog-prometnog-rezima) and once approved, the system should recognize your licence plate at checkpoints.

Penalties for violations can be high. Please carefully follow the guidance of warning screens and traffic lights on key roads in the city regarding approaching and entering the restricted traffic zone.

### **Hiking and mountain climbing**

Get advice from local guides and experts if you are walking or hiking in the mountains anywhere in Croatia. Comprehensive safety guidelines are available on the [**Croatian Mountain Rescue Service websit**e](https://www.hgss.hr/en/) and their social media on X at [**@HrvatskaGSS**](https://x.com/HrvatskaGSS)and Facebook at [**@HrvatskaGSS**](https://www.facebook.com/HrvatskaGSS).

Do not hike alone or use unmarked paths. Inform your family, friends or a reliable contact that you are going hiking. Check the weather forecast before hiking as weather can change suddenly in the mountains and temperatures can get very low overnight. Make sure you wear suitable clothes, footwear and sun protection, have plenty of fresh water and the necessary equipment such as food, first aid kit, a fully charged mobile phone and a torch.

If you need emergency assistance, call the emergency number 112 for help from the Croatian Mountain Rescue Service.
