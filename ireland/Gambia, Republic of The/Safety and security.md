## Safety and security

### **Crime**

The Gambia is a popular destination with tourists and most visits there are trouble-free. However, cases of robbery, burglary and assault do occur, including at tourist sites and hotels frequented by foreign nationals.

Travellers are advised to be vigilant about street crime like pickpocketing and bag-snatching. To reduce your personal risk:

* avoid carrying large sums of money or valuables
* use a hotel safe to store passport and valuables whenever possible
* be alert when withdrawing cash from ATMs
* stay vigilant in crowded areas
* use crossbody bags worn in front, and secure valuables in front pockets or hidden money belts
* be particularly careful after nightfall and avoid walking alone
* take care of your belongings when arriving at and leaving hotels
* keep a copy of your passport’s photo page and your visa or entry stamp in a separate place

As foreign nationals, Irish citizens must carry ID such as a passport. The police will usually accept a printed copy.

### Scams

We advise travellers to be vigilant about potential scams. In particular, be cautious of young men locally known as ‘bumsters’ who approach tourists, particularly on beaches, offering help or to act as local guides. They may offer to take you on tours into neighbouring Senegal. It is unlikely they will follow the correct immigration procedures. This could result in you being detained by immigration authorities. They may also ask for money.

Be polite but firm in refusing unwanted help or attempts at conversation.

### Protests

Presidential elections are scheduled to be held in The Gambia in December 2026. There is a higher risk of unrest and violence during election periods. Avoid large gatherings and protests in public areas, follow the advice of local authorities, and monitor local media for up-to-date information.

### Southern border

The Casasmance region in Senegal, to the south of The Gambia, continues to experience conflict involving armed groups, separatists and clashes between the military and the MFDC (Movement of the Democratic Forces of Casamance).

There has been sporadic fighting along The Gambia’s southern border in the recent past. Stay on main roads and do not travel at night if travelling near or across the southern border.

Land mines remain present in certain areas around the southern border. Only drive on paved roads and follow the advice of local authorities.

### Transport and road safety

If you are planning to drive in The Gambia, you should be extremely careful. Road conditions and driving practices can be hazardous, including in Banjul. Accidents are frequent and are often caused by poor driving, badly maintained vehicles, a lack of traffic signs, and inadequate lighting.

During the rainy season from June to September, torrential rains can cause floods and landslides which damage roads.

We strongly advise against undertaking long overland journeys, especially at night. Driving after dark is dangerous because of poorly lit roads and vehicles without lights. Avoid all road travel near or across the southern border at night.

### Security checkpoints

Security checkpoints operate in and around the capital Banjul and are common on all major routes. They are not always well sign-posted. Take care when approaching them and expect your vehicle to be searched if you are stopped by security forces.

### Illegal drugs

There is a zero tolerance towards illegal drugs in the The Gambia. This includes importing, exporting, or possession of drugs. Do not accept packages on behalf of anyone without knowing the contents.

Sentences for those found in possession of drugs can be severe, including fines or prison sentences.
